LEARNLN_01concrete
What Are Equal Parts?
When a whole is divided into parts that are ALL the same size, we call them EQUAL PARTS. If any part is bigger or smaller than the others, the parts are UNEQUAL. Example: A sandwich cut exactly in half → 2 equal parts. A cake cut with one large piece and one small piece → unequal parts.
🚩 mark
LEARNLN_02pictorial
Names for Equal Parts
The number of equal parts tells us the name: • 2 equal parts → halves (each part = one half) • 3 equal parts → thirds (each part = one third) • 4 equal parts → fourths (each part = one fourth) • 6 equal parts → sixths (each part = one sixth) • 8 equal parts → eighths (each part = one eighth)
🚩 mark
LEARNLN_03abstract
Equal Parts Must Cover the Whole
Equal parts must: (1) all be the same size, AND (2) fit together to make the whole with no gaps or overlaps. Example: A square divided into 4 equal triangles — each triangle is one fourth. All 4 triangles together make the whole square.

📖 개념 학습 (5문항)
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_01concrete
What Are Equal Shares?
When you divide a whole into parts that are the same size, you make equal shares. If a pie is cut into 4 equal pieces, each piece is 1/4 of the pie.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_02concrete
Different Shapes, Same Fraction
You can show 1/2 in many ways: cut a circle down the middle, cut a rectangle across the center, or cut a square diagonally. As long as both parts are the same size, each part is 1/2.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_03pictorial
Equal vs. Unequal Shares
Not every cut makes equal shares. If a rectangle is cut into 3 parts but one part is bigger, those are NOT equal shares. All parts must be the same size to be named as fractions.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_04pictorial
Sixths and Eighths
When a whole is divided into 6 equal parts, each part is 1/6 (one sixth). When divided into 8 equal parts, each part is 1/8 (one eighth). The more equal parts you cut, the smaller each part becomes.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_05abstract
Naming Equal Shares
To name equal shares: (1) Count the total number of equal parts — that is the denominator. (2) Count the parts you are describing — that is the numerator. For 1 out of 6 equal parts, write 1/6.

LEARNLRN_01concrete
What Is a Unit Fraction?
A unit fraction names 1 equal part of a whole. It always has 1 as the numerator. Examples: 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_02concrete
Numerator and Denominator
Every fraction has two numbers. The top number is the numerator — it tells how many parts you are counting. The bottom number is the denominator — it tells how many equal parts are in the whole. In 1/4, the numerator is 1 and the denominator is 4.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_03pictorial
Smaller Denominator = Larger Part
When a whole is divided into fewer parts, each part is bigger. So 1/2 is larger than 1/4, and 1/4 is larger than 1/8. Imagine cutting a pizza into 2 slices vs. 8 slices — 2 slices are much bigger!
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_04pictorial
Reading a Unit Fraction
To read a unit fraction, look at the denominator. The fraction 1/3 is read 'one third' — the whole is split into 3 equal parts and you are counting 1 of them. 1/6 = 'one sixth', 1/8 = 'one eighth'.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_05abstract
Writing Unit Fractions
To write a unit fraction from a description: (1) Count the total equal parts → that is the denominator. (2) Write 1 as the numerator (always for unit fractions). Example: 'one of 6 equal parts' → 1/6.

LEARNLRN_01concrete
Naming Fractions
To name a fraction: count the shaded (or chosen) parts — that is the numerator (top). Count the total equal parts — that is the denominator (bottom). Example: 3 out of 4 parts shaded = 3/4.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_02concrete
More Than a Unit Fraction
A unit fraction has 1 as the numerator (like 1/4). When more than 1 part is shaded, the numerator is greater than 1 (like 2/4 or 3/4).
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_03pictorial
The Whole as a Fraction
If ALL parts are shaded, the fraction equals the whole. 4/4 = 1 whole, 6/6 = 1 whole, 8/8 = 1 whole.

LEARNLRN_01concrete
Fractions on a Number Line
A number line from 0 to 1 can be divided into equal parts, just like a shape. If you divide it into 4 equal lengths, the marks show 0/4, 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, and 4/4.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_02concrete
Locating a Fraction
To find a fraction on a number line: 1) Look at the denominator — it tells how many equal lengths between 0 and 1. 2) Count from 0 the number of lengths shown by the numerator. Example: 3/4 → divide into 4 parts, count 3 from 0.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_03pictorial
Endpoints
0/4 is at 0 (the start). 4/4 is at 1 (the end). Any fraction with 0 as the numerator equals 0. Any fraction where numerator = denominator equals 1.

LEARNLRN_01concrete
Fractions That Equal 1
When the numerator and denominator are the same, the fraction equals 1 whole. Examples: 2/2 = 1, 3/3 = 1, 4/4 = 1, 8/8 = 1.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_02concrete
Fractions Greater Than 1
When the numerator is greater than the denominator, the fraction is greater than 1. Example: 8/4 means you have 8 fourths. Since 4 fourths = 1 whole, 8 fourths = 2 wholes. So 8/4 = 2.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_03pictorial
Whole Numbers as Fractions
Any whole number can be written as a fraction. Multiply the whole number by the denominator to find the numerator. Example: 3 = 3 × 4 / 4 = 12/4. Also 3 = 6/2 = 9/3 = 12/4 = 24/8.

LEARNLRN_01concrete
Fraction of a Group
A fraction can name part of a group, not just part of a shape. The denominator is the total number of items. The numerator is how many items match. Example: 3 out of 5 fish are goldfish → 3/5 of the fish are goldfish.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_02concrete
Finding the Numerator
Count how many items match the description. If there are 8 stickers and 5 are stars, the numerator for 'star stickers' is 5 → 5/8.
🚩 mark
LEARNLRN_03pictorial
Part NOT Described
To find the fraction that is NOT the described part, subtract the numerator from the denominator. 5/8 are stars → 8 − 5 = 3 are not stars → 3/8.
