LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is Area?
AREA is the amount of space INSIDE a flat shape. We can think of area as how much paper, paint, or tile is needed to cover a shape with no gaps. Area answers the question 'how much space does this shape cover?'
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Unit Squares
A UNIT SQUARE is a square with all sides 1 unit long. We use unit squares to MEASURE area. Common unit squares: 1 cm × 1 cm = 1 square cm. 1 inch × 1 inch = 1 square inch. 1 m × 1 m = 1 square meter.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Covering Without Gaps or Overlaps
To find area, we cover a shape with unit squares. RULES: (1) NO GAPS — every part of the shape must be covered. (2) NO OVERLAPS — squares cannot stack on each other. Every unit square counts as 1 square unit of area.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Counting Unit Squares
After covering a shape with unit squares (no gaps, no overlaps), count them. The total count is the AREA in SQUARE UNITS. Example: a rectangle covered by 12 unit squares has area = 12 square units.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05pictorial
Area Units
Area is measured in SQUARE UNITS — square centimeters (sq cm), square inches (sq in), square meters (sq m), square feet (sq ft). The word 'square' is important: it means we are measuring area, not length.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Area vs Length
LENGTH is how far across (one direction): cm, m, inches. AREA is how much surface (two directions): sq cm, sq m, sq inches. A line has length but no area. A rectangle has both length AND width — together they tell you about area.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Bigger Shapes Need More Squares
A bigger shape needs MORE unit squares to cover it. A small sticky note might be 9 sq cm. A book cover might be 400 sq cm. A school playground might be 1,000 square meters. Bigger shape = more area = more unit squares.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today you learned: (1) AREA = space inside a flat shape. (2) UNIT SQUARE = 1×1 square. (3) Cover with NO GAPS, NO OVERLAPS. (4) Count unit squares to find area. (5) Area uses SQUARE UNITS (sq cm, sq m, etc.). (6) Bigger shape = more area.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
Counting Strategy
To find the area of a shape covered with unit squares: COUNT them. We can count one by one for any shape, but for rectangles, we use a faster way: rows × columns.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Rows and Columns
A ROW of unit squares goes ACROSS (left to right). A COLUMN goes UP and DOWN. A rectangle has rows AND columns of unit squares. The total = (number of rows) × (number of squares in one row).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Multiplication Shortcut for Area
For rectangles: AREA = ROWS × COLUMNS (in unit squares). Example: 5 rows × 4 columns = 20 unit squares = 20 sq units. This is much faster than counting all 20 squares one at a time.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Counting Irregular Shapes
For shapes that are NOT rectangles (like L-shapes, T-shapes), count the unit squares one by one. Or split the shape into smaller rectangles, find each area, then ADD.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Same Shape, Different Counts?
If two students cover the SAME shape with NO gaps and NO overlaps, they both get the SAME area count. Area is fixed for a shape — it doesn't change based on who counts.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Smaller Squares = More Squares
If you use SMALLER unit squares, you need MORE of them to cover the same shape. A rectangle that fits 12 unit squares of 1×1 might fit 48 squares of 1/2 × 1/2 — but the area is still the same in standard units.

LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Use a Strategy
Choose the best counting strategy: (1) RECTANGLE → rows × columns. (2) IRREGULAR → count one by one OR split into rectangles. (3) Always check no gaps and no overlaps.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
Why Multiplication Works
When you cover a rectangle with unit squares, you make rows AND columns. The total = rows × squares-per-row. A rectangle with LENGTH L and WIDTH W has L × W unit squares — so its area = L × W.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Length and Width
Rectangles have a LENGTH (the longer side, usually) and a WIDTH (the shorter side). Some books also say SIDE LENGTHS. To find area, you need both side lengths.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Area Formula for Rectangle
FORMULA: Area = Length × Width (or A = L × W). Example: a 6 cm by 4 cm rectangle has area = 6 × 4 = 24 sq cm. The formula is a shortcut so we don't have to count every unit square.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Area Formula for Square
A square is a special rectangle where length = width. So Area = side × side. Example: a square with side 5 cm has area = 5 × 5 = 25 sq cm.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Units in Area
When sides are in cm, area is in sq cm. When sides are in m, area is in sq m. ALWAYS write the unit with the area: 24 sq cm, 50 sq m, 120 sq ft. Don't mix units (don't multiply 5 cm × 3 m).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Order Doesn't Matter
Length × Width = Width × Length. A 4×7 rectangle has the same area as a 7×4 rectangle: both are 28 sq units. This is the COMMUTATIVE property.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Find a Missing Side
If you know the area and one side, you can find the other side by dividing. Example: a rectangle has area 24 sq cm and length 6 cm. Width = 24 ÷ 6 = 4 cm.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today: (1) Area of rectangle = Length × Width. (2) Area of square = side × side. (3) Always include 'square' units. (4) Order doesn't matter (4×7 = 7×4). (5) To find a missing side: Area ÷ known side.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
Area as Repeated Addition
When you cover a rectangle with unit squares, each ROW has the same number of squares. Adding the squares row by row is REPEATED ADDITION. Example: 4 rows of 5 squares each: 5 + 5 + 5 + 5 = 20.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Repeated Addition Becomes Multiplication
Repeated addition is the same as multiplication. 5 + 5 + 5 + 5 = 4 × 5 = 20. Multiplication is just a faster way to write repeated addition.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Arrays of Unit Squares
An ARRAY is a rectangular arrangement of items in equal rows. The unit squares of a rectangle form an array. Counting an array by rows and adding gives the total = AREA.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Adding Areas of Two Rectangles
If a shape can be split into two rectangles (no overlap), find each area separately, then ADD them together. Total area = Area 1 + Area 2.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Why Adding Works
Two rectangles side by side don't share unit squares. So if Rectangle 1 has 12 squares and Rectangle 2 has 8 squares, the total is 12+8=20 squares. Adding works whenever there are no overlaps.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Tiling a Rectangle
TILING means covering a rectangle with unit squares (or square tiles) without gaps or overlaps. Tiling helps us see why area = rows × columns and why repeated addition works.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Choose Your Tool
(1) Repeated addition: best for small numbers or when learning. (2) Multiplication: faster, especially for larger rectangles. (3) Split + add: best for irregular shapes that can be cut into rectangles.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today: (1) Area can be found by repeated addition (row + row + row...). (2) Repeated addition = multiplication. (3) Split irregular shapes into rectangles, find each area, then ADD. (4) Tiling is the visual model.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is the Distributive Property?
The DISTRIBUTIVE PROPERTY says: a × (b + c) = (a × b) + (a × c). It lets you SPLIT one of the numbers into two parts, multiply each part, and ADD the results.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Why Use It?
Sometimes 6 × 8 is hard to recall. Split: 6 × 8 = 6 × (5 + 3) = 30 + 18 = 48. Splitting into facts you KNOW makes hard multiplication easier.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Area Picture of Distributive Property
Imagine a 6 × 8 rectangle. Cut it into a 6 × 5 rectangle and a 6 × 3 rectangle. Total area = (6×5) + (6×3) = 30 + 18 = 48 sq units. Same as the original.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Choose Smart Splits
Pick splits that use facts you know well. Common choices: 5 + something. Example: 7 × 9 = 7 × (5 + 4) = 35 + 28 = 63.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Multiple Ways to Split
There are MANY ways to split. 8 × 7 = 8 × (5+2) = 40+16 = 56. Or 8 × 7 = 8 × (4+3) = 32+24 = 56. Or 8 × (6+1) = 48+8 = 56. All give the same answer.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Split Either Factor
You can split EITHER number. 6 × 8 = (5+1) × 8 = 5×8 + 1×8 = 40+8 = 48. The distributive property works either way.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Apply to Area
When a rectangle is too big to recall directly, split it visually into 2 smaller rectangles whose areas you know. Add the areas. This is finding area using the distributive property.

Area vs Perimeter — The Big Difference
AREA = the SPACE INSIDE a shape. Measured in SQUARE units. Use multiplication. PERIMETER = the DISTANCE AROUND the outside. Measured in regular (length) units. Use addition.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Different Units
Area: SQUARE units (sq cm, sq m). Perimeter: regular length units (cm, m). The two measurements answer different questions, so they use different units.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
When to Use Area
Use AREA when the question is about COVERING or FILLING. Examples: How much paint to cover a wall? How big is the floor for carpet? How much grass on a field?
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
When to Use Perimeter
Use PERIMETER when the question is about going AROUND or BORDERING. Examples: How much fence around a yard? How much string for a frame? How much trim around a window?
