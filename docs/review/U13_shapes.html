LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is a Polygon?
A POLYGON is a closed figure made of straight sides. Two rules: (1) it must be CLOSED — all sides connect with no gaps. (2) every side must be STRAIGHT — no curves allowed. Examples of polygons: a triangle, a square, a stop sign. NOT polygons: a circle (curved), a half-open shape (not closed).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Sides of a Polygon
Each STRAIGHT line segment that forms a polygon is called a SIDE. Count the sides by tracing around the polygon and counting each straight segment. A triangle has 3 sides. A square has 4 sides. A stop sign has 8 sides.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Vertices — The Corners
A VERTEX is the point where two sides meet — what you might call a CORNER. The plural is VERTICES. A triangle has 3 vertices (3 corners). A pentagon has 5 vertices. Important rule: the number of vertices ALWAYS equals the number of sides.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Angles — Inside Each Corner
An ANGLE is formed at each vertex by the two sides that meet there. Every polygon has the SAME number of angles as sides. A triangle has 3 angles. A hexagon has 6 angles. So for ANY polygon: number of sides = number of vertices = number of angles.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05pictorial
Right Angles — The Square Corners
A RIGHT ANGLE looks like the corner of a book, a window, or a piece of paper. It is a perfect square corner. Squares and rectangles have 4 right angles each. Some polygons have NO right angles (like a regular triangle). Some have just one or two.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Naming Polygons by Sides
Polygon names tell you the number of sides: TRIANGLE = 3 sides ('tri' = 3). QUADRILATERAL = 4 sides ('quad' = 4). PENTAGON = 5 sides ('penta' = 5). HEXAGON = 6 sides ('hex' = 6). OCTAGON = 8 sides ('oct' = 8). Tip: an octopus has 8 arms — octagon has 8 sides.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Polygon or Not? — The Two Tests
To check if a figure is a polygon, ask TWO questions: (1) Is it CLOSED? If there is any gap or opening, it is NOT a polygon. (2) Are ALL sides STRAIGHT? If even ONE side is curved, it is NOT a polygon. A circle fails test 2 (curved). An open V-shape fails test 1 (not closed).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today you learned: (1) A polygon is a CLOSED figure with all STRAIGHT sides. (2) SIDES are the straight segments. (3) VERTICES are the corners where sides meet. (4) ANGLES are formed at each vertex. (5) For any polygon: sides = vertices = angles. (6) A RIGHT ANGLE is a square corner. (7) Names: 3=triangle, 4=quadrilateral, 5=pentagon, 6=hexagon, 8=octagon.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is an Attribute?
An ATTRIBUTE is a special feature of a shape — like number of sides, kind of angles, or whether sides are equal. Examples of attributes: 'has 4 sides', 'has a right angle', 'has all sides equal'. We can sort shapes into groups by their attributes.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Classify Means Sort into Groups
To CLASSIFY shapes is to put them in groups by something they have in common. Example: Put all polygons with 3 sides in one group, and all polygons with 4 sides in another group. The 3-sided group has triangles. The 4-sided group has squares, rectangles, rhombuses, and trapezoids.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Classify by Number of Right Angles
Another way to classify polygons is by their angles. SQUARE: 4 right angles. RECTANGLE: 4 right angles. RIGHT TRIANGLE: 1 right angle. REGULAR PENTAGON: 0 right angles. So squares and rectangles share the attribute '4 right angles'.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Classify by Side Lengths
We can sort polygons by whether their sides are equal. ALL SIDES EQUAL: square (4 equal sides), rhombus (4 equal sides), equilateral triangle (3 equal sides). NOT ALL SIDES EQUAL: rectangle that is not a square, scalene triangle, most trapezoids.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Shapes Can Have Many Attributes
One shape can have many attributes at the same time. A SQUARE has: 4 sides, 4 right angles, all sides equal. So a square belongs to several groups: 'has 4 sides', 'has 4 right angles', and 'has all sides equal'. The same shape can fit into more than one group.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Some Groups Live Inside Other Groups
Every SQUARE is also a RECTANGLE — because a square has 4 sides and 4 right angles, just like rectangles. But not every rectangle is a square (a rectangle that is 5 cm by 3 cm has 4 right angles but is not a square). So the 'square group' fits INSIDE the 'rectangle group'.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Two Shapes Share an Attribute When…
Two shapes SHARE an attribute when both shapes have that feature. Square and rectangle both have 4 right angles. Square and rhombus both have 4 equal sides. Triangle and hexagon both are closed figures with all straight sides (both are polygons). Look for what they have in common.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today you learned: (1) An ATTRIBUTE is a feature of a shape. (2) To CLASSIFY is to sort shapes into groups by attributes. (3) Common attributes: number of sides, number of right angles, equal side lengths. (4) One shape can have many attributes. (5) A square is also a rectangle, because every square has 4 right angles.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
Quadrilateral — The 4-Sided Family
A QUADRILATERAL is any polygon with 4 sides and 4 angles. Squares, rectangles, rhombuses, parallelograms, and trapezoids are all quadrilaterals. The word 'quadrilateral' just means '4-sided polygon' — it does not say anything special about the sides or angles.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02pictorial
Square — All Equal AND All Right
A SQUARE is a special quadrilateral with TWO attributes at once: (1) all 4 sides are equal, AND (2) all 4 angles are right angles. Examples: chessboard squares, floor tiles, sticky notes that are perfectly square. If a shape has 4 equal sides AND 4 right angles, it must be a square.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Rectangle — 4 Right Angles
A RECTANGLE is a quadrilateral with 4 right angles. Its OPPOSITE sides are equal (top = bottom, left = right), but the long sides do not have to match the short sides. Examples: a door, a book cover, a phone screen. Note: every square IS also a rectangle (because squares have 4 right angles too).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Rhombus — All 4 Sides Equal
A RHOMBUS is a quadrilateral with all 4 sides equal in length. Its angles do not have to be right angles. A rhombus often looks like a tilted, diamond-shaped square. Note: every square IS also a rhombus (because squares have 4 equal sides too).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05pictorial
Parallel Sides
Two sides are PARALLEL if they go in the same direction and never cross — like two railroad tracks. PARALLEL SIDES are sides of a polygon that are parallel to each other. We use this idea to define more quadrilaterals: parallelograms and trapezoids.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Parallelogram — 2 Pairs of Parallel Sides
A PARALLELOGRAM is a quadrilateral with 2 pairs of parallel sides — both pairs of opposite sides are parallel. Squares, rectangles, and rhombuses are ALL parallelograms (each has 2 pairs of parallel sides). The opposite sides of a parallelogram are also equal in length.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Trapezoid — At Least 1 Pair of Parallel Sides
A TRAPEZOID is a quadrilateral with AT LEAST ONE pair of parallel sides. Some trapezoids have only one pair of parallel sides (the other two sides slant in different directions). Note: in some textbooks, parallelograms count as special trapezoids.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today you learned the quadrilateral family: (1) QUADRILATERAL = any 4-sided polygon. (2) SQUARE = 4 equal sides + 4 right angles. (3) RECTANGLE = 4 right angles. (4) RHOMBUS = 4 equal sides. (5) PARALLELOGRAM = 2 pairs of parallel sides. (6) TRAPEZOID = at least 1 pair of parallel sides. A square is a special rectangle, rhombus, AND parallelogram!

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Does It Mean to Compare?
To COMPARE two shapes is to look at what is the SAME and what is DIFFERENT. Same = shared attributes. Different = an attribute one has but the other doesn't. Example: A square has 4 right angles AND a rectangle has 4 right angles → SAME. A square has all 4 sides equal, but a (non-square) rectangle does not → DIFFERENT.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Same: 4 Sides
All quadrilaterals have 4 sides. So when you compare any two quadrilaterals, '4 sides' is always SAME. Squares, rectangles, rhombuses, parallelograms, and trapezoids all have 4 sides. The number of sides does not help you tell them apart.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Compare Square and Rectangle
SAME: 4 sides, 4 right angles, 2 pairs of parallel sides. DIFFERENT: A square has all 4 sides equal; a non-square rectangle has 2 long and 2 short sides. Both are special quadrilaterals — every square is a rectangle, but not every rectangle is a square.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Compare Square and Rhombus
SAME: 4 sides, all 4 sides equal, 2 pairs of parallel sides. DIFFERENT: A square has 4 right angles; a (non-square) rhombus does not. Every square is a rhombus, but not every rhombus is a square.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05pictorial
Compare Rectangle and Rhombus
SAME: 4 sides, 2 pairs of parallel sides. DIFFERENT: A rectangle has 4 right angles, but a (non-square) rhombus does not. A rhombus has 4 equal sides, but a (non-square) rectangle does not. The two shapes have different special attributes.

LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Compare Parallelogram and Trapezoid
SAME: 4 sides, both have at least 1 pair of parallel sides. DIFFERENT: A parallelogram has 2 pairs of parallel sides; a trapezoid (in 3rd grade) has only 1 pair. So a parallelogram has more parallel sides than a trapezoid.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is Partitioning?
To PARTITION a shape is to divide it into parts. When the parts are all the same size, we call them EQUAL PARTS. Examples: cutting a pizza into 4 equal slices, cutting a chocolate bar into 6 equal pieces. Each piece is a part of the WHOLE.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Equal Parts Must Be the Same Size
EQUAL parts means same SIZE. If you cut a pizza so one slice is bigger and another is smaller, those are NOT equal parts. To make equal parts, every piece must be exactly the same size — like 4 perfectly matching pizza slices.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Halves — 2 Equal Parts
When a shape is partitioned into 2 EQUAL parts, each part is a HALF. The fraction for one half is 1/2 (one out of two). Two halves together make a whole. Example: a circle cut down the middle makes 2 halves.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Thirds and Fourths
THIRDS = 3 equal parts. Each is 1/3. THIRDS + THIRDS + THIRDS = WHOLE. FOURTHS = 4 equal parts. Each is 1/4. Four fourths make a whole. Example: a rectangle cut into 4 equal rows shows fourths.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05pictorial
Sixths and Eighths
SIXTHS = 6 equal parts. Each is 1/6. EIGHTHS = 8 equal parts. Each is 1/8. The MORE equal parts a shape is divided into, the SMALLER each part becomes. A shape cut into 8 parts has smaller pieces than the same shape cut into 4 parts.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Different Ways to Partition the Same Shape
You can partition a square into 4 equal parts in different ways: (1) 4 equal rows, (2) 4 equal columns, (3) 2 rows and 2 columns. All three give 4 equal parts. The shape of each part may LOOK different (rectangle vs square), but each part still equals 1/4 of the whole.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
More Parts → Smaller Pieces
When the SAME WHOLE is partitioned into more parts, each part is SMALLER. Order from biggest to smallest piece for the same whole: 1/2 > 1/3 > 1/4 > 1/6 > 1/8. The denominator (bottom number) tells you how many equal parts — bigger denominator means smaller piece.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today you learned: (1) PARTITION = divide a shape into parts. (2) Equal parts must be the SAME SIZE. (3) Names: 2=halves, 3=thirds, 4=fourths, 6=sixths, 8=eighths. (4) Each part is named by the fraction 1/n where n is the total number of equal parts. (5) More parts = smaller pieces. (6) The same shape can be partitioned in different ways.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is a Unit Fraction?
A UNIT FRACTION is a fraction with 1 as the NUMERATOR (top number). Examples: 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8. Unit fractions describe ONE piece of an equally-partitioned whole. The DENOMINATOR (bottom number) tells you how many equal parts the whole is divided into.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Equal Parts Have Equal Areas
When a shape is partitioned into n EQUAL parts, every part has the SAME AREA. Example: a rectangle divided into 4 equal parts — each part has the same area, even if the parts look different (one might be a strip, another a square).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Each Part Is 1/n of the Area
If a shape is partitioned into n equal parts, the AREA of each part is 1/n of the whole's area. Examples: 2 equal parts → each has area 1/2 of the whole. 4 equal parts → each has area 1/4. 8 equal parts → each has area 1/8.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Add Unit Fractions to Make a Whole
Adding all the equal parts gives back the WHOLE. 1/2 + 1/2 = 2/2 = whole. 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 4/4 = whole. 1/8 + 1/8 + ... eight times = 8/8 = whole. The number of unit fractions needed equals the denominator.

LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Bigger Denominator → Smaller Piece
When you compare unit fractions of the SAME WHOLE: a bigger denominator means a smaller piece. Order from biggest to smallest: 1/2 > 1/3 > 1/4 > 1/6 > 1/8. Why? More equal parts means each part is smaller.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Combine Unit Fractions
When you put two or more unit fractions of the SAME size together, you get a NON-UNIT fraction. Example: 1/4 + 1/4 = 2/4. 1/8 + 1/8 + 1/8 = 3/8. The denominator stays the same; you add the numerators.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Find Each Part's Area
If you know the area of the WHOLE shape, you can find the area of each equal part. Divide the whole's area by the number of parts. Example: a rectangle with area 24 sq cm divided into 4 equal parts — each part has area 24 ÷ 4 = 6 sq cm.

All quadrilaterals have 4 sides. Special types: SQUARE (4 equal sides + 4 right angles), RECTANGLE (4 right angles), RHOMBUS (4 equal sides), PARALLELOGRAM (2 pairs parallel), TRAPEZOID (1 pair parallel). Squares are a special type of rectangle, rhombus, AND parallelogram.

LEARNLEARN_03pictorial

PARTITION = divide a shape into equal parts. Names: 2=halves, 3=thirds, 4=fourths, 6=sixths, 8=eighths. Each equal part is 1/n of the whole, where n is the total number of parts.

LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Unit Fractions and Area
When a shape is partitioned into n equal parts, each part has 1/n of the whole's area. Example: a 24 sq cm rectangle in 4 parts → each part is 24 ÷ 4 = 6 sq cm. Bigger denominator = smaller piece.
