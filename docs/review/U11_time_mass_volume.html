LEARNLEARN_01concrete
Parts of a Clock
An ANALOG clock has two hands: the SHORT one is the HOUR HAND. The LONG one is the MINUTE HAND. Numbers 1-12 are around the clock face. (Some clocks also have a thin SECOND hand.)
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Hour and Minute Numbers
1 HOUR = 60 MINUTES. Each NUMBER on the clock face = 5 MINUTES (because 60÷12=5). So the minute hand on '3' means 15 minutes. On '6' means 30 minutes. On '9' means 45 minutes.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Reading the Hour
The HOUR HAND tells you the hour. If the hour hand points BETWEEN 4 and 5, the hour is 4 (because the hour hasn't reached 5 yet). Always say the LOWER hour.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Reading the Minutes
Count by 5s starting from 12. 12=0 min, 1=5 min, 2=10 min, 3=15 min, 4=20 min, 5=25 min, 6=30 min, 7=35, 8=40, 9=45, 10=50, 11=55. If the minute hand is between numbers, count 1-by-1 (5 small ticks per number).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05pictorial
Nearest Minute
To tell time to the NEAREST MINUTE, count by 5s up to the nearest small tick, then add the extra ticks. Example: minute hand 1 tick past the 7 → 35 + 1 = 36 minutes.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Writing Time
Write time as HH:MM. Example: 3 hours and 25 minutes = 3:25. The hour goes first, then a colon, then the minutes (always two digits — like 3:05, not 3:5).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
A.M. and P.M.
A.M. = before noon (midnight to noon, 12:00 A.M. to 11:59 A.M.). P.M. = after noon (noon to midnight, 12:00 P.M. to 11:59 P.M.). Morning = A.M. Afternoon/evening = P.M.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today: (1) Hour hand short, minute hand long. (2) Each number = 5 minutes. (3) Hour = lower of two numbers the hour hand is between. (4) Count by 5s for minutes; add extra ticks for nearest minute. (5) Write as HH:MM. (6) A.M. = morning, P.M. = afternoon/evening.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is Elapsed Time?
ELAPSED TIME is how much time has PASSED between a START TIME and an END TIME. Example: a movie starts at 2:00 and ends at 3:30. Elapsed time = 1 hour and 30 minutes = 90 minutes.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Use a Number Line
Draw a NUMBER LINE. Mark the START time on the left, END time on the right. HOP from start to end in friendly steps (hours or minutes). Add up the hops.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Hop Strategy
(1) HOP TO THE NEXT HOUR first. Example: 2:45 → 3:00 = 15 min. (2) HOP IN HOURS until close to the end. (3) HOP IN MINUTES the rest of the way.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Same Hour Strategy
If start and end are in the SAME hour, just SUBTRACT the minutes. Example: 4:10 to 4:35 → 35 - 10 = 25 minutes.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Across Hours Strategy
When you cross hour boundaries, use 'count up' from start: minutes to next hour, plus full hours, plus minutes to end. Example: 1:50 to 3:10. 1:50 → 2:00 = 10 min. 2:00 → 3:00 = 60 min. 3:00 → 3:10 = 10 min. Total: 80 min.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Convert Hours to Minutes
1 hour = 60 minutes. 2 hours = 120 minutes. 3 hours = 180 minutes. To convert: hours × 60.

LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Total in Minutes or Hours+Minutes
An elapsed time of '1 hour 30 minutes' is the same as '90 minutes'. You can write the answer either way.

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
What Is Mass?
MASS is how much MATTER an object has. Heavier objects have more mass. Mass is measured with a BALANCE or SCALE. The basic units are GRAM (g) and KILOGRAM (kg).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Gram
A GRAM (g) is a small unit. Examples: a paperclip is about 1 g. A coin is about 5 g. A small bag of chips is about 30 g. Grams measure LIGHT objects.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03concrete
Kilogram
A KILOGRAM (kg) is a bigger unit. 1 KILOGRAM = 1,000 GRAMS. Examples: a textbook is about 1 kg. A small dog is about 5 kg. A bag of rice is about 2 kg. Kilograms measure HEAVIER objects.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Choose the Right Unit
USE GRAMS for: small light items (coin, eraser, candy). USE KILOGRAMS for: heavier items (backpack, watermelon, person, dog).
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05pictorial
Estimate Mass
ESTIMATE = a smart guess. To estimate: compare to something you know. A grape is about 5 g. An apple is about 200 g. A textbook is about 1 kg.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Convert Between Grams and Kilograms
TO CONVERT KG TO G: multiply by 1000. Example: 4 kg = 4000 g. TO CONVERT G TO KG: divide by 1000. Example: 5000 g = 5 kg.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Use a Balance
A BALANCE compares two masses. Place the object on one side, mass-pieces on the other. When the sides BALANCE (level), the masses are equal. The mass-pieces' total = the object's mass.
🚩 mark

Mass Problems Use Add and Subtract
When TOTAL mass is needed → ADD. When DIFFERENCE is needed → SUBTRACT. Read the problem carefully to know which operation.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Multiply for Equal Groups
When you have GROUPS of equal mass, MULTIPLY. Example: 4 apples × 200 g each = 800 g total.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Divide for Equal Sharing
When TOTAL mass is split equally, DIVIDE. Example: 12 kg of rice ÷ 4 bags = 3 kg per bag.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Match Units
Always check that all measurements are in the SAME unit before computing. Convert if needed: 1 kg = 1000 g.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Keywords That Hint Operations
TOTAL, COMBINED, ALTOGETHER → ADD. LEFT, FEWER, DIFFERENCE → SUBTRACT. EACH, GROUPS OF → MULTIPLY. SHARED EQUALLY, EACH GETS → DIVIDE.

LEARNLEARN_03concrete
Liter
A LITER (L) is a bigger unit. 1 LITER = 1,000 MILLILITERS. Examples: a water bottle ≈ 1 L. A milk carton ≈ 2 L. A bathtub ≈ 200 L.

Choose the Right Unit

Convert L to mL

LEARNLEARN_01concrete
Volume Problems Like Mass
Volume word problems use the same operations as mass: ADD for total, SUBTRACT for difference/left, MULTIPLY for equal groups, DIVIDE for equal sharing.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_02concrete
Match Units First
Always check that volumes are in the same unit (all mL or all L). Convert if needed: 1 L = 1000 mL.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_03pictorial
Combining Volumes
When you POUR multiple containers together: ADD their volumes. Example: 200 mL + 300 mL = 500 mL.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_04pictorial
Pouring Out
When you POUR OUT from a container: SUBTRACT what was poured. Example: 1 L = 1000 mL. Pour out 400 mL → 1000-400=600 mL left.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_05abstract
Equal Servings
If you have a TOTAL volume to divide into equal servings: DIVIDE. Example: 2 L into 4 cups → 500 mL each.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_06abstract
Multiplying Equal Volumes
If many containers each hold the same volume: MULTIPLY. Example: 6 bottles × 500 mL each = 3000 mL = 3 L.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_07abstract
Read Carefully and Estimate
Read the problem carefully. Estimate the answer first. If a problem says 'one glass', the answer should be in mL or 1 L, not 100 L.
🚩 mark
LEARNLEARN_08abstract
Lesson Summary
Today: (1) Use add, subtract, multiply, divide. (2) Match units first. (3) Adding combines; subtracting takes away. (4) Multiply for equal groups, divide for equal sharing. (5) Estimate before computing.
